Unmasking the Power Games in Burkina Faso under Ibrahim Traoré

Luc and the Machine June 30, 2025

Geopolitical and Propaganda Dynamics Under Ibrahim Traoré in Burkina Faso

 

 

From Coup to “Savior”: Traoré’s Rise and Grand Promises

Captain Ibrahim Traoré seized power in Burkina Faso’s second coup of 2022, deposing a fellow junta leader amid public outrage over spiraling jihadist violencereuters.comreuters.com. At just 34 years old, he catapulted onto the world stage as Africa’s youngest head of state, vowing to “reclaim sovereignty” and end the security emergency that previous regimes and their French allies had failed to resolvem.facebook.comreuters.com. Traoré’s inaugural address declared the nation in peril – beset by terrorism, crumbling infrastructure, and social crises – and pledged swift action on a transition to democracy by July 2024issafrica.org. Yet, that promise soon gave way to an extended military rule: after breaking with regional bloc ECOWAS, the junta reset the timeline so that Traoré “may remain in his position until at least 2029”issafrica.orgissafrica.org.

Despite this power grab, Traoré rapidly gained fervent popular support, especially among Burkinabè youth. Many hailed him as a pan-African savior in the mold of 1980s revolutionary Thomas Sankarafpri.org. He tapped into deep frustrations with both the jihadist insurgency and the perceived failures of Western-backed governments, striking a chord with citizens desperate for change. In early 2023, opinion surveys showed an unprecedented acceptance of military rule – 66% of Burkinabè said they approved, up from just 24% a decade earlierissafrica.orgissafrica.org. Traoré’s image as a humble man-of-the-people further fueled this approval: he pointedly reversed lavish pay raises for officials and continued to draw only his captain’s salary, a populist gesture in a country weary of elite corruptionissafrica.org. To many, the young captain embodied a new era of proud self-reliance – a chance for “Africans to handle their own affairs” free from foreign tutelageissafrica.org.

Yet Traoré’s youth and inexperience (he was a low-ranking artillery officer only weeks before the coupreuters.com) raise questions about his ability to deliver on sweeping promises. Pro-junta media and social networks have propagated an almost messianic aura around him, touting grand ambitions: to electrify half of Africa, achieve fuel and food self-sufficiency, build new schools and infrastructure, and keep Burkina Faso’s gold wealth at home. In reality, these goals would be herculean for any leader, let alone one governing amid war and sanctions. What lies beneath this heroic narrative? A closer look reveals a web of manipulations – from local propaganda to geopolitical maneuvering – that complicate the story of Traoré’s “miracle” rule.

Propaganda, Populism, and the Cult of Traoré

Figure: Supporters of Captain Ibrahim Traoré rally in Ouagadougou on October 2, 2022, waving Russian flags and even torching the French Embassy. Such scenes underscored a surge of anti-French, pro-Russian sentiment after Traoré’s coupreuters.comreuters.com. These demonstrations portrayed Traoré as a liberator from neo-colonial influence – an image carefully amplified by a burgeoning propaganda machine.

From the moment he took power, Traoré has benefited from an aggressive state-directed propaganda campaign that verges on an alternate reality. In the digital arena, his supporters have engineered viral content portraying him as a beloved visionary leader. Remarkably, in mid-2025 a wave of deepfake AI-generated music videos appeared, using the synthetic voices of global stars like Beyoncé and R. Kelly to sing praises of Traoré in gospel-like tonesfpri.org. These clips – obviously fake, yet seen by millions – implore divine protection for Traoré and celebrate him as a God-sent protector of the people. Such cult-of-personality messaging, with quasi-religious undertones, has fostered a messianic image of the young president among his followers. Never mind that the real Beyoncé or other celebrities almost certainly know nothing of Burkina Faso’s leader – in Traoré’s curated online universe, international icons and ordinary Africans alike seemingly worship him.

State-controlled media and pro-junta influencers drum a constant beat of pan-Africanist and anti-imperialist rhetoric. Traoré is cast as “walking in the footsteps” of Burkina Faso’s post-colonial hero Thomas Sankara, echoing Sankara’s themes of self-reliance and defiance of Western dominancefpri.org. His speeches rail against France and extol new partnerships with “respectful” allies (hinting at Russia and China)lansinginstitute.orgfpri.org. The regime’s messaging blames almost all of Burkina’s woes on foreign exploitation – a powerful narrative in a country where memories of French colonial rule and economic control run deep. Legitimate grievances underpin this narrative: for example, France’s decades-long military presence failed to stem terrorist attacks, and the French-backed CFA franc currency is seen as a shackle keeping West African economies subservientfpri.orgfpri.org. Traoré taps into these sentiments, portraying himself as the champion who will “break all ties that keep us in slavery”fpri.org.

At the same time, dissenting voices have been muzzled to maintain an image of nationwide adulation. Since Traoré took power, independent media and civil society in Burkina Faso have been heavily repressed. The junta has suspended critical foreign broadcasters and even forcibly conscripted or “disappeared” outspoken journalists and activists, in effect silencing most domestic oppositionreuters.comreuters.com. This coercion, combined with a low national literacy rate and tight control of information, means the state narrative faces little challengefpri.orgfpri.org. Within Burkina, many citizens genuinely see Traoré as the country’s savior – or at least give him the benefit of the doubt – because alternative perspectives are scarce and dangerous to voice. As one Sahel analyst observed, the junta’s “propaganda arm actively pursues anti-democratic thought,” choking off healthy debate in favor of a unifying savior mythfpri.org.

It is important to note that Traoré’s government has pursued some concrete nationalist projects behind the propaganda. For instance, the regime has moved to assert greater control over Burkina Faso’s gold – the country’s chief export. Traoré nationalised at least two gold mines and inaugurated a national refinery to process gold domestically rather than exporting unrefined ore to Europeissafrica.org. In 2024 the junta adopted a new mining code to raise state royalties and enhance local ownership in the industrytrt.globaltrt.global. The government formed a state mining company that took over two mines from a London-based firm, and pressured foreign operators to increase the state’s share in projects (one Australian company quickly boosted Burkina’s stake from 10% to 15% under this pressure)trt.globaltrt.global. Traoré’s ministers have also touted investments in agriculture (e.g. new cotton processing facilities) and infrastructure like a new international airportissafrica.org. In the energy sector, facing chronic electricity shortages, the junta has courted non-Western partners: for example, China provided a €45 million loan for a new solar power plant to reduce Burkina’s reliance on imported electricityafrica-energy-portal.orgafrica-energy-portal.org. There are even plans for an Atomic Energy Agency to explore nuclear power for developmentneimagazine.com. All these moves bolster the narrative that Traoré is working to fulfill his promises of energy sovereignty, resource nationalism, and industrial revival.

The reality, however, remains daunting. While propaganda videos boast of “groundbreaking mega-projects” and a coming golden age, on the ground Burkina Faso is beset by daily bloodshed, economic strain, and the limitations of an isolated military regime. Many of Traoré’s promises lack technical and financial underpinning, leading skeptics to question how a beleaguered 36-year-old captain can electrify and enrich one of the world’s poorest countries virtually overnight. The answer may be that he cannot – at least not alone. Which raises the question: who is really pulling the strings in Burkina Faso’s new course? To answer that, one must examine the shadowy contest of external powers now playing out in the country.

Russia’s Hand: From Disinformation to Mercenaries

One of the most significant “hidden manipulations” in Burkina Faso’s turmoil is the role of Russia. After taking charge, Traoré dramatically pivoted Burkina Faso’s alliances away from its former colonizer France and toward Moscowthedefensepost.comthedefensepost.com. This was not a spontaneous swing, but rather the culmination of years of influence-building by the Kremlin in the region. Covert Russian information campaigns had been laying groundwork in Burkina Faso well before Traoré’s coup. In fact, as early as 2021, analysts observed pro-Russian fake news sites, Facebook groups, and WhatsApp networks seeding the idea that France was the enemy and Russia the saviorwashingtonpost.comwashingtonpost.com. A meme circulating in Burkina Faso at the time baldly declared: “In six months, Putin cleaned up all the terrorists, while for 50 years under French influence, terrorists killed 100 people a day.”washingtonpost.com This grossly distorted claim exemplified how Russian disinformation painted Moscow as a miracle-worker against jihadism and France as either inept or complicit.

Such messaging had a powerful effect. When Traoré struck in September 2022, civilians poured into the streets waving Russian flags and denouncing France, essentially echoing the narratives Moscow had promulgatedreuters.comreuters.com. It is now clear that Russian influence operations helped fuel the anti-French, pro-coup atmosphere that enabled Traoré’s rise. A recent analysis notes that “Russian influence campaigns aimed to foster anti-French sentiment” in Burkina Faso and neighboring Sahel states, with at least 19 distinct Russian disinformation operations targeting Burkina, Mali, and Niger by early 2024fpri.orgfpri.org. These coordinated campaigns stoked public anger at France’s military presence and “helped unseat Western…missions in the Sahel”, paving the way for juntas that are friendly to Moscowfpri.orgfpri.org. In short, Russia spent years laying an online trap, and the Burkinabè military walked into it, coup d’état in handwashingtonpost.comwashingtonpost.com.

Once Traoré was in power, Russia moved from the virtual domain to the physical. By early 2023, Burkina Faso expelled French troops and welcomed Russian security assistancelansinginstitute.orglansinginstitute.org. Unofficially, this has meant the arrival of Wagner Group mercenaries or their successors. In late January 2024, for example, a first contingent of about 100 Russian fighters landed in Burkina Faso, reportedly under the banner of the newly formed “Russia’s Africa Corps” – a rebranded Wagner network now controlled by the Russian Defense Ministryosw.waw.plosw.waw.pl. Hundreds more were expected to followosw.waw.plosw.waw.pl. This deployment was part of a broader Kremlin strategy to “fill the security vacuum” left as France retreated from the Sahel, and to entrench Russian influence militarily, politically, and economically across the regionosw.waw.plosw.waw.pl.

Traoré’s government has openly courted Moscow’s support. In May 2025, Traoré visited President Vladimir Putin in the Kremlin, praising Russia’s military technology and resilience under sanctionsthedefensepost.comthedefensepost.com. He called for deeper military and scientific partnerships and noted Burkina Faso had “many projects” underway with Russia’s defense ministrythedefensepost.com. While he diplomatically refrained from directly requesting Russian troops (knowing Russia is tied up in Ukraine)thedefensepost.com, Traoré clearly sought training, weapons, and strategic counsel from Moscow to fight the insurgency and fortify his regimethedefensepost.comlansinginstitute.org. Putin, for his part, promised to help “suppress radical groups” in Burkina and to continue assisting the juntathedefensepost.comthedefensepost.com. On paper, the common goal of “combating terrorism” was underscored by both leadersthedefensepost.com. But behind this counterterrorism rhetoric lies a host of other motives.

For Russia, Burkina Faso is a pawn in a larger geopolitical chess match. By siding with Traoré’s junta, the Kremlin secures another ally in Africa and undermines Western influence in a former French sphere. Strategically, Burkina (along with Mali and now Niger) forms part of a bloc of military regimes aligned with Moscow, offering Russia diplomatic support (for example, at the U.N.) and potential economic rewardslansinginstitute.orglansinginstitute.org. Foremost among those rewards are natural resources. Burkina Faso is Africa’s fourth-largest gold producer, and gold is an attractive asset for Russia – especially under sanctions – either to acquire directly or to use as payment-in-kind for security services. Analysts note that Traoré’s pivot to Russia likely involves resource deals, as seen elsewhere (Mali reportedly pays Wagner mercenaries with gold concessions)lansinginstitute.orglansinginstitute.org. Indeed, the risks of this relationship include that “Russia has a history of extracting resources (gold, diamonds, etc.) from African nations under military agreements,” potentially meaning Burkina’s new “friends” could help themselves to its gold wealth under the guise of assistancelansinginstitute.org.

Another motive is geopolitical positioning. With Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger forming a new “Alliance of Sahel States” after their coups, Russia sees an opportunity to foster a pro-Moscow security bloc in the heart of West Africaosw.waw.pl. This could put pressure on pro-Western governments in the region and even give Russia leverage over strategic resources like Uranium (Niger) and trade routes to the Atlantic. In essence, Africans are being pulled into a proxy struggle: the old empire (France/West) versus the resurgent empire (Russia/China). As one foreign policy expert observed, Russia has effectively used disinformation and military aid “to establish a new Sahel alliance” that evicted French forces and UN peacekeeperswashingtonpost.comwashingtonpost.com. Burkina Faso is a key piece of that puzzle.

Crucially, Russia’s help does not come as a charitable enterprise. While Traoré pitches the partnership as anti-imperialist, he may find that swapping patrons means trading one form of dependence for another. Russian mercenary involvement often buys regime security at a steep price. The Wagner Group (or its successors) can bolster Traoré’s personal safety – providing reliable guards and cracking down on coup plots – which is valuable to a young leader who has already survived several foiled putsch attempts against himissafrica.org. However, this insurance can make the regime “dependent on a foreign power,” undercutting its nationalist imagelansinginstitute.org. There is also the ethical and strategic cost: Wagner’s counterinsurgency record in Africa is brutal, with widespread atrocities documented in countries like the Central African Republic (which prompted rare public protests against Wagner in 2025)fpri.orgfpri.org. Their presence can inflame local conflicts and drive more civilians into the arms of jihadists, even as state forces claim short-term victoriesfpri.orgfpri.org. In Mali, since the junta invited in Wagner, terrorism incidents have significantly increased, U.S. officials noted, contrary to the promise of improved securityfrance24.com. A similar trend may be emerging in Burkina: despite the new Russian “partnership,” 2023 was the country’s deadliest year of the insurgency yet, with over 8,000 people killed in militant attacks – a “severe escalation” by any measurereuters.comreuters.com.

In summary, Russia has skillfully manipulated Burkina Faso’s crisis to its advantage. It helped shape the information environment that brought Traoré to power, is now arming and advising his forces, and stands to gain influence and riches from the arrangement. The people of Burkina Faso find themselves encouraged to fight “terrorists” at home and to blame France for their woes, all while embracing Russia as a liberator. But as one analyst warned, this path risks “encouraging junta rule [that] establishes a path to nowhere,” where civilian abuses continue and jihadism spreads uncheckedfpri.orgfpri.org. Are Burkinabè merely trading one empire for another? Increasingly it appears so – the old French patron has been shown the door, and a new patron from the east has stepped in, draped in the rhetoric of liberation but pursuing its own power projection.

Jihadist War: Conflict Reality vs. Conspiracy

Underpinning Burkina Faso’s turmoil is its horrific jihadist insurgency – a reality that both the regime and foreign players have manipulated for their ends. By the numbers, Burkina Faso is now the epicenter of global terrorism, outranking even Iraq and Afghanistan in impactfpri.org. Since 2015, Islamist militant groups have killed thousands and seized swathes of territory in the north and east. The main factions are Jama’at Nasr al-Islam wal-Muslimin (JNIM), an Al-Qaeda affiliate, and the local branch of Islamic State (ISGS). These militants exploit grievances of marginalized communities, weak state presence, and spillover from conflicts in Mali and Libya. They have grown so bold that they’ve attacked major towns, using drones and even anti-aircraft guns against the Burkinabè armyfpri.org. In short, the jihadists are a very real and formidable threat, not a mere phantom. Traoré’s chief justification for his coup was the previous government’s failure to stop this carnagereuters.comreuters.com, and his regime has since thrown itself into an all-out war against the militants – often with scorched-earth tactics that have drawn human rights criticismsreuters.com.

However, in the battle for narrative, the nature and origin of these jihadist groups have become subjects of speculation and conspiracy. Traoré’s supporters and many ordinary Burkinabè harbor profound suspicions that foreign powers are secretly backing the Islamist rebels. These suspicions did not arise in a vacuum; they were fueled by a mix of observable events and disinformation. For example, Mali’s junta (a close ally of Traoré) has openly accused France of aiding terrorists: in 2022 Malian officials wrote to the UN Security Council accusing France of clandestine flights delivering arms to jihadist groupsvoanews.com. While France vehemently denied such claims, the accusations resonated with local populations already convinced that France’s counterterrorism mission was a failure – perhaps deliberately so. Traoré himself has implied that the French military presence was counterproductive, saying French forces were “unwilling to target certain groups” and thus allowed terrorists to thrivefpri.orgfpri.org. Such statements feed the popular belief that the former colonial power had an ulterior motive to keep Burkina Faso weak and dependent.

Even more extreme are theories involving the United States or Israel as puppet-masters of jihadist outfits. In the fever swamps of the internet (and in some pan-Africanist or Middle Eastern “alternative” media), one finds claims that Al-Qaeda, ISIL and the like are “shadow organizations” set up by Western intelligence or Mossad. These conspiracy theories often cite how jihadist movements have at times indirectly benefited Western geopolitical goals (for instance, weakening unfriendly regimes), or point to the historical arming of mujahideen in the Cold War. In the Sahel context, no credible evidence has emerged that the U.S. or Israel directly control groups like JNIM or ISGS – these groups have local roots and allegiances to global jihadist ideologies. Nonetheless, such ideas flourish in an information vacuum where distrust of official narratives runs high. When villagers see sophisticated weapons in militant hands, they ask how ragtag rebels obtained them – fertile ground for rumors of foreign sponsors. And indeed, Western and Israeli actors have been covertly involved in Burkina Faso – but on the opposite side of the fight. A Washington Post investigation revealed that an Israeli private firm, Percepto, was hired in 2021 to help then-President Kaboré counteract online subversionwashingtonpost.com. Percepto’s analysts uncovered a massive Russian-driven campaign to destabilize the Sahel governmentswashingtonpost.comwashingtonpost.com. They created fake Facebook personas to infiltrate pro-Russia groups and wage an info-war against Kremlin-backed disinformationwashingtonpost.comwashingtonpost.com. In other words, far from running the jihadists, Israeli operatives were trying to expose and combat the influence operations enabling Traoré’s coup. This little-known chapter underscores the complexity of the “hidden hand” at work: multiple foreign actors – Russians, Western governments, Israelis, regional players – are all vying to shape outcomes in Burkina Faso, often behind the scenes.

That said, the perception of a grand conspiracy retains power. Traoré has leaned into it at times to rally support. State propaganda frequently insinuates that the jihadists are proxies of outside forces bent on Burkina’s ruin, rather than indigenous rebels with local grievances. This serves to absolve the junta of sole responsibility for ongoing violence (after all, if foreign devils are pulling the strings, how could Traoré magically end the war?) and to justify seeking new alliances (cue Russia) to replace perfidious France. The unfortunate consequence is that such thinking can lead to brutal suspicion of ordinary civilians as “foreign-backed” enemies, fueling reprisals and ethnic killings in conflict zones. Indeed, both jihadis and the army have massacred villagers suspected of collaborating with the other sidereuters.com, driving a vicious cycle of revenge that foreign powers can then exploit via propagandafpri.org.

So, is this war a charade run by a single “Empire” pulling all strings? The evidence suggests a more tangled reality. Burkina Faso’s conflict is not stage-managed by one hidden puppeteer, but it is being manipulated by many different actors for their own ends. Islamist militants genuinely seek to carve out territory and impose their ideology, but global powers seize upon their presence to advance strategic narratives. Russia uses the persistence of terrorism to sell itself as the better security partner (even as its methods may worsen the violence)fpri.org. France and the U.S. highlight jihadist atrocities to justify counterterrorism operations – until they are kicked out, after which they warn that Russian involvement is making things worsefrance24.com. Regional governments like Mali’s and Burkina’s leverage conspiracy claims to delegitimize domestic opponents (branding critics or certain communities as terrorist-collaborators) and to break from Western dependency. In this fog, the people of Burkina Faso are effectively caught in a proxy war and an information war simultaneously. They bleed and die in attacks and reprisals, while on YouTube and Facebook they are told to cheer Traoré as a messiah and Putin as a friend, or alternately to fear that Traoré has sold them out to new masters. The truth – that the jihadist threat is fueled by decades of local state neglect, climate-driven desperation, and regional instability – gets buried under blame games and propaganda.

Beyond the Rhetoric: Interests and Motives of the “Hidden Hands”

Looking behind the scenes of Traoré’s rule, we find a convergence of motives among various “hidden hands” – be it the Burkinabè junta, Russian operatives, or even Western actors – that often centers on one thing: resource power and strategic control. Burkina Faso may be poor in GDP terms, but it is rich in resources: aside from gold, it has significant deposits of zinc, manganese, phosphates, and other minerals, plus untapped reserves of metals like nickel and vanadiumissafrica.org. Control over these resources is a high prize. Traoré has explicitly framed his revolution in these terms, declaring “every responsible government must control its resources.” He touts moves to reclaim Burkina’s “vast gold reserves” from foreign hands as part of a new pan-Africanist sovereigntytrt.globaltrt.global. This resonates with Burkinabè who saw international companies extract billions in gold over years while local communities remained destitutetrt.global. Indeed, Burkina Faso’s status quo was a textbook case of resource extraction with scant local benefit – something Traoré is trying to upend, at least in rhetoric.

The junta’s mining policy shifts in 2023-2024 – raising royalties, creating a state miner (SOPAMIB), nationalizing select mines – sent shockwaves through Western business circlestrt.globaltrt.global. They also drew warnings from Western officials. Notably, the head of U.S. Africa Command, Gen. Michael Langley, accused Traoré of using Burkina’s gold revenue to enrich his military cronies at the people’s expense (though he offered no evidence for that claim)trt.global. Such statements hint at Western concern that the junta might be funneling gold to fund its own patronage or to pay Russian mercenaries under the table. (Wagner’s model in other countries has been to take direct shares of gold production, so the suspicion is not far-fetchedlansinginstitute.org.) For Russia, enabling Burkina to cut out Western mining firms could be doubly beneficial: it kicks out competitors and potentially gives Moscow-affiliated entities a slice of the pie under new contracts. China, too, has interests here – it has been quick to offer financing for energy and infrastructure, positioning itself as a partner as France retreatsafrica-energy-portal.orgafrica-energy-portal.org. Chinese loans and projects (like the solar plant) dovetail with Traoré’s vision of “non-Western” development assistance without political strings. Of course, Chinese loans have their own strings (debt and influence), underscoring that Burkina’s new friends seek profit and leverage, not just altruism.

Another layer is the monetary and financial decoupling Traoré has pursued. Echoing other francophone African nationalists, he railed against the CFA franc currency system – which ties local currencies to the euro and requires African states to park reserves in Paris – as “slavery”fpri.orgfpri.org. In early 2024, he proclaimed Burkina would “break all ties” of this kindfpri.org. Along with Mali and others, Burkina Faso is exploring exits from the CFA zone, though implementing a new currency or aligning with another system (perhaps a BRICS currency or other mechanism) is fraught with challenges. This move attacks a core of French economic influence (often called “neo-colonial”). It’s telling that while Traoré talks up these plans, Russian and Chinese media cheer him on – likely because weakening France’s post-colonial structures creates a vacuum they can fill with their own economic systems (e.g. integrating countries into Chinese-led finance or using commodities-for-loans deals). The motive of these bigger players is clear: gain a stronger foothold in West Africa’s markets and resources, and if possible, flip a former Western client state into their client.

In these maneuvers, one might argue that Traoré himself is both an agent and a subject of manipulation. On one hand, he is driving a genuine agenda of “resource nationalism” that aligns with his personal ideology and domestic popularity – fulfilling a kind of Sankara-esque legacy of economic independencetrt.globaltrt.global. On the other hand, to enact that agenda he has made himself reliant on new outside sponsors (Russia for security, China for funds), which could compromise the very sovereignty he champions. This duality is often the tragedy of African revolutions: lofty ambitions to expel one empire sometimes lead to dependency on another patron, especially when pursued by regimes that lack robust institutions or broad international support.

Meanwhile, France and its Western allies are hardly passive. Though militarily outmaneuvered for now, they maintain economic pressure. Burkina Faso’s aid from the EU and U.S. has been largely frozen since the coups, and the country’s withdrawal from regional bodies like ECOWAS and the loss of IMF/World Bank support (which Traoré pointedly rejectedissafrica.org) mean it faces financial isolation. Western governments have also quietly supported neighboring coastal states (Ghana, Côte d’Ivoire, etc.) to contain any southward spread of Sahel instability. There is a real sense of a Great Game being played over Burkina Faso: an internal conflict that has drawn in competing international “architects” each with their motives – be it counterterrorism, resource extraction, currency dominance, or ideological influence.

Conclusion: A New Revolution or Another Pawn on the Board?

The situation in Burkina Faso under Ibrahim Traoré is a blend of genuine grassroots yearning, calculated propaganda, and high-stakes geopolitical chess. On the surface, the narrative is uplifting: a young captain inspired by a revolutionary past rises to cast off an old empire and restore pride to his people. Traoré has skillfully used that narrative, cultivating an almost cult-like following through promises of transformation and shows of defiance against France and the West. He has taken concrete steps – nationalizing mines, expelling foreign troops, rejecting IMF loans – that symbolize a break with the old orderissafrica.orgissafrica.org. This has made him a hero figure not only in Burkina Faso but across a segment of African youth who see in him a bold rejection of neo-colonialism. The almost fanatical public enthusiasm (manifested in rallies with Russian flags and even AI-generated songs of praise) attests to the powerful appeal of his messagereuters.comfpri.org.

However, peel back the propaganda, and a darker picture emerges. Traoré’s rule has seen a sharp authoritarian turn at home, with opponents detained or silenced and civil liberties erodedreuters.comreuters.com. The security situation for ordinary Burkinabè has, by objective measures, worsened: terrorist attacks have increased in lethalityreuters.com, and the army’s heavy-handed reprisals have at times terrorized the very population it’s meant to protectreuters.com. Millions are displaced within the country, and large rural areas remain outside government control, plagued by hunger and fear. In this sense, the junta has so far failed to deliver the primary relief it promised – peacereuters.comreuters.com. The grand development projects circulated on social media remain mostly aspirational or embryonic; it will take years to know if a new gold refinery, a solar plant, or agricultural reforms truly improve daily life. As one African commentator noted, “to reverse 60 years of structural theft of resources will take more than three years”trt.global, cautioning that early signs of progress are modest and must overcome the usual pitfalls of corruption and mismanagement.

On the international front, Burkina Faso has traded an open-ended French military presence for a closer embrace of Moscow – a move that might buy Traoré short-term military aid and political cover, but carries serious long-term riskslansinginstitute.orglansinginstitute.org. Already, Russian involvement has not visibly improved security, and if anything, the conflict’s brutality has intensified. The junta’s alignment with Russia (and to a degree China) has also isolated it from many of its African neighbors and global partners, risking economic pain through lost trade and aidlansinginstitute.org. Traoré insists this is the price of true independence, framing it as Africa’s fight to “develop without the West’s loans and conditionalities.”issafrica.org But whether Burkina Faso can actually thrive in this new alignment is uncertain. If the jihadist threat continues to grow despite Russian help, the junta may find itself in a corner – having burned bridges with the West, yet unable to deliver security via its new friends.

Crucially, the notion of a single “Empire” orchestrating events in Burkina Faso from behind the scenes does not hold up to scrutiny. What we see instead is a collision of multiple agendas: a tug-of-war where every major power projects its designs onto Burkina’s turmoil. Russia pushes anti-colonial solidarity, but arguably pursues its own neocolonial footholdlansinginstitute.orglansinginstitute.org. France and the U.S. speak of democracy and stability, but their past policies (military operations that failed to defeat jihadists, economic arrangements that benefited foreign companies) helped trigger the backlash that brought the junta to powerfpri.orgfpri.org. Local actors like Traoré and his allies seek to maximize Burkina’s autonomy and their own power, yet they operate in a context of dependency and under the constant threat of insurgency and internal coupslansinginstitute.orglansinginstitute.org. Rather than a grand global conspiracy with everyone in on it, the evidence suggests an environment of shifting opportunism: each player – foreign or domestic – is leveraging the chaos to achieve their aims, sometimes cooperating, sometimes at odds.

For the people of Burkina Faso, this means their struggle is tragically being fought on two fronts. On one front, brave volunteers and soldiers battle jihadist raiders in the bush, trying to protect villages and reopen schools. On the other front, an information and influence war rages over their minds and loyalties – where propaganda portrays those battles in ideological terms and may conceal as much as it reveals. In this climate, Ibrahim Traoré is both a symbol of hope and a potential instrument of deception. He might sincerely want to be the next Sankara, delivering Burkina Faso from despair, and he has energized a nation with that hope. But his youth and fervor make him just as likely to be used by canny outsiders or swallowed by the same authoritarian pitfalls as past strongmen. As a regional expert aptly put it, “whether he will be remembered as a revolutionary hero or another military strongman depends on how he navigates Burkina Faso’s ongoing challenges.”lansinginstitute.org The coming years will test if Traoré’s revolution is real or just a charade – a nationalist theater that distracts from a new era of exploitation under different masters.

In conclusion, what’s “really going on” in Burkina Faso is a complex interplay of internal desperation and external manipulation.
